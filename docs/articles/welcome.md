# Little Brain Project (LPB)
## Exploring the evolutionary roots of intelligence

### Overview

The **Little Brain Project (LPB)** explores how neural circuits give rise to intelligent behavior, using neural evolution as a guiding framework. It seeks to understand not only how brain mechanisms are implemented, but also why their organization emerged in the light of evolution. Beyond retracing what may have happened, LPB speculates on how it could have unfolded — not to imagine different outcomes, but to better understand the one that led to us. By exploring possible intermediate steps and mechanisms, the project aims to illuminate how evolution might have assembled the neural architectures that make intelligence possible.

Rooted in the tradition of **cybernetics**, LPB seeks to understand brains by **building them**, giving them physical expression through a real, embodied robot.  
Through minimalist agents, LPB explores how intelligence arises from the continuous dialogue between nervous system, body, and environment.

This site serves as a **working record** of the project — a place where progress is marked by functional or conceptual increments rather than dates. Each entry reflects the development of the robot and the reasoning behind specific implementation choices. Unlike evolution, time, or an agenda, this site can — and will — be rewritten to provide an account of understanding as it unfolds.

### From Minimal Creatures to Complex Minds

We begin with minimal, reactive agents — simple sensor-to-motor architectures that already exhibit lifelike, purposeful behavior.  
Heavily inspired by **Valentino Braitenberg’s _Vehicles: Experiments in Synthetic Psychology_**, these foundational systems are implemented in real robots, allowing us to observe and analyze how behavior emerges directly from neural organization and embodiment.

From these starting points, LPB investigates how increasingly sophisticated neural mechanisms can support:

- Navigation and spatial orientation  
- Reflexes and behavioral coordination  
- Learning and adaptation  
- Internal representations and models  

While much of the work focuses on the neural implementation of individual functions, the **ultimate goal** of the project is to understand **how these mechanisms integrate** — how perception, action, and memory come together to form a unified, coherent creature.  
Here lies a major strength of the project: each task is never tackled in isolation. It is always integrated with existing functions — guided by evidence when possible, or by reasoned coherence when necessary.

What makes a creature *a creature*?

### Inspiration from Nature

The project draws strong inspiration from **insect neuroscience**, where compact yet capable brains solve complex problems through elegant neural architectures.  
By connecting insights from insect research with **embodied computational models**, LPB seeks to uncover the **general design principles** underlying adaptive intelligence in both biological and artificial systems.

### Why “Little Brain”?

Small does not mean simple.  
Even the tiniest nervous systems demonstrate powerful organizational principles that underpin cognition and control.  
In this bottom-up approach, the project complements other research areas that explore the dynamics of large neural networks.  
The **Little Brain Project** celebrates these natural designs — and seeks to reveal how intelligent behavior evolved from the simplest neural beginnings.

### Meet MinimU

**MinimU** is the Little Brain Project’s embodied agent — a small, wheeled, wireless robot that brings minimalist neural architectures to life.  
The robot includes onboard sensors for IMU data, wheel rotation, voltage, and battery level, and can be expanded with additional wireless modules.

#### Communication and Control

- **Computer communication:** wireless interface for data exchange and control  
- **Bonsai integration:** programming through the **Bonsai** visual reactive framework, based on **ReactiveX**, enabling real-time sensorimotor processing  
- **Sensors:** IMU, encoders, voltage, and current feedback  
- **Actuation:** differential wheel drive with feedback control  

Although not constrained by it, MinimU is typically programmed in **Bonsai**, allowing behavior to emerge through continuous feedback between sensing and actuation.  
Through MinimU, we study how simple circuits, embodied in motion and perception, can give rise to the first glimmers of adaptive and integrated behavior.
